Kinematics is a subfield of physics, developed in classical mechanics, that describes the motion of points, boides(objects) without considering the forces that cause them to move. Included with this a several equations that accurately describe motion as long as acceleration is constant.

There are 5 main equations to use when trying to use kinematics to describe the motion of objects. Below are the full forms of the 5 kinematic equations, as well as their variable forms:

1. Total displacement = (Average velocity over the trip (final velocity - initial velocity)) * time expired OR \trianglex = vt
2. final velocity = initial velocity + (acceleration of object * time expired) OR Vf = Vi + at
3. (average velocity = initial velocity + final velocity)/2 OR V(avg) = (Vf + Vi)/2
4. total displacement = (initial velocity * time elapsed) + 0.5(acceleration of object)(time elapsed squared) OR \trianglex = Vot + 1/2(a)(t)^2
5. 2(acceleration of object)(total displacement) = (final velocity squared) - (initial velocity squared) OR 2a\trianglex = (Vf)^2 - (Vi)^2

When given 3 variable throughout any of the 5 equations, through substitution, we are able to calculate any of the unknown variables.
